verified Document 1 The Why Philosophy, intent, and what makes Zenny categorically different 1.1 The problem with every 
existing bot Every existing trading bot is built on a common architectural assumption: that price action expressed as 
OHLCV candles is the primary input. Indicators are derived from that price action. RSI measures the speed of recent 
price movement. MACD measures the relationship between moving averages of price. Even the most advanced algorithmic 
systems are applying increasingly complex transformations to historical price data and waiting for signals to emerge. 
This has a structural problem. Price is a lagging record of what already happened. Every indicator built on price 
inherits that lag. By the time the signal appears, the best entry is already behind you. Zenny is built on a different 
premise entirely. Price does not move randomly. Price moves toward liquidity. Liquidity — clusters of resting orders, 
stop losses, liquidation thresholds — is the cause of price movement. Price action is the effect. Zenny reads the 
cause directly and positions itself at the destination before the journey begins. 1.2 The Braid — a completely new 
visual object The Braid is not a better candlestick chart. It is a different kind of visual object built for a specific 
purpose: verifying that a bot is behaving with strategic integrity. A candlestick chart is optimised for human analysts 
making real-time decisions. The Braid is optimised for answering one question — did the bot do what it was designed 
to do. The Braid has three temporal zones. The left section is history: the price wire moving through the pool 
landscape with all past interactions visible. The now line is the present moment. The right section splits into two 
panels — one for the pool above, one for the pool below — each showing what Zenny expects to happen when price 
arrives. Figure 1: The Braid — price wire on left, now line dividing present, two tendency panels on right. Last real 
candle on dominant arm. Ghost candle shows projected sweep. 1.3 Why the rectangle and not the line Technical analysis 
has drawn support and resistance as horizontal lines for a century. A line implies a precise price at which the market 
will reverse. Zenny draws rectangles. Each rectangle is constructed from actual wick extremes: the ceiling is the 
highest wick that touched and rejected the level. The floor is the lowest wick. The centre line is the body cluster. 
The rectangle also has a lifecycle — born on first tick cluster detection, active while scoring above 60/100, 
weakening as wick rejections tighten, dead when a candle engulfs it. When it dies it flips polarity. Resistance becomes 
support. The level persists with a new role. Figure 2: Pool lifecycle — birth marker, wick boundaries, touch 
interactions, death marker, polarity flip from support to resistance. 1.4 Why the angle and not a regime declaration 
Zenny reads regime from the geometry of the wire itself. The angle of the price wire over the last 14 candles is the 
regime. It updates with every new candle close. Gann identified the meaningful thresholds in 1935: below 14 degrees is 
noise — no trade. 14 to 26.25 is accumulation. 26.25 to 45 is ranging. 45 to 63.75 is trending. Above 63.75 is 
breakout or unsustainable. Figure 3: Four strategy scenarios derived from wire angle. Gap between arms represents 
velocity toward dominant pool — wide gap = trending, narrow gap = accumulation. 1.5 Intelligence map — all inputs 
to the Braid Zenny monitors and weights dozens of signals simultaneously. No human can do this in real time. The 
intelligence map shows how every data source flows through the system to produce the Braid visual and trade decisions. 
Figure 4: Zenny intelligence map — all signals flowing from raw data through pool grading, regime classification, and 
pull scoring to visual output. 1.6 Three-layer architecture Every pool on the trading timeframe exists within a higher 
timeframe context. The daily and 4H establish the permanent backdrop. The 1H is the active layer where trades are 
planned. The 15m is the execution layer where entry candles form. These are not three separate analyses — they are 
three lenses on the same underlying reality. Figure 5: Three-layer architecture — landscape (daily/4H), trading (1H), 
execution (15m). Higher timeframes establish context; lower timeframes time entry. 1.7 Why integrity score and not 
P&amp;L P&amp;L is deeply misleading. A bot gambling randomly in a bull market shows positive P&amp;L. A bot executing 
a sound strategy in adverse conditions shows negative P&amp;L. Neither number tells you whether the bot is doing what 
it was designed to do. The integrity score measures something different: of all valid setups that existed on a given 
day, what fraction did Zenny correctly identify and act on — either by taking the trade or by intentionally skipping 
it with a logged reason. This was the exact diagnostic that caught the original bot&apos;s failure. P&amp;L masked the 
problem completely. The integrity score would have caught it in minutes. 1.8 The relationship to the existing codebase 
Zenny is a rework, not a rebuild. The following components are explicitly preserved: The auto-research framework based 
on Karpathy&apos;s self-testing methodology — this is the fundamental verification backbone The Coinglass liquidation 
heatmap integration — feeds ScoreLiquidationCluster directly The panel and dashboard scaffolding — the Braid 
renders within this The existing scoring and grading logic — reworked to match specification, not replaced Document 2 
Functional Specification Every function — rules, inputs, outputs, call relationships 2.1 Architecture overview 
Zenny&apos;s functional architecture is a pipeline with eight layers: data ingestion, tick processing, candle analysis, 
pool identification, pool scoring, strategy classification, pull calculation, and trade and visual output. Every 
function has defined inputs and defined outputs. RegimeGuard runs as a gate before any trade output — no exceptions. 
2.2 Data ingestion Function Rule / Logic Test Criteria FetchCandles Inputs: symbol, timeframe, lookback. Calls Binance 
klines endpoint. Returns [{timestamp,open,high,low,close,volume}]. Validates: no gaps, timestamps ordered, all values 
non-null. Caches on symbol+timeframe+last_timestamp. Output matches TradingView for same symbol/timeframe within 0.01%. 
No missing candles. No duplicates. Updates cache correctly on new candle close. FetchOrderBook Inputs: symbol, 
depth_limit (default 500). Calls Binance /api/v3/depth. Returns {bids:[[price,qty]], asks:[[price,qty]]}. Updates via 
WebSocket. Bids sorted descending, asks ascending. Spread matches Binance live feed within 100ms. Depth at each price 
matches raw API. Updates within 1 second of order book change. FetchTicks Inputs: symbol, start_timestamp. Subscribes 
to Binance trade stream WebSocket. Returns stream of {timestamp,price,qty,side}. Side=BUY if market order lifted ask, 
SELL if hit bid. Every trade in raw stream appears in output. No duplicates. Timestamps ordered. Volume matches trade 
size exactly. FetchLiquidationData Inputs: symbol, timeframe. Calls Coinglass API. Returns {price_level: 
liquidation_usd} map. Caches 5 minutes. Values match manual Coinglass query for same symbol/window within 5% tolerance. 
Cache invalidates correctly after 5 minutes. FetchFundingRate Inputs: symbol. Calls Binance futures 
/fapi/v1/fundingRate. Returns {rate, next_funding_time}. Caches 1 minute. Matches Binance futures funding rate endpoint 
for same timestamp exactly. NormaliseTimeframe Inputs: candles_1H array. Aggregates every 4 candles: open=first.open, 
high=max(highs), low=min(lows), close=last.close, volume=sum. Output length = floor(input/4). Synthetic 4H candle 
matches native 4H from exchange within 0.01%. CacheHistoricalData Inputs: key(symbol+timeframe+timestamp), data. TTL = 
one candle period. Second call with same key returns cached data without API hit. No API hit on second call. Cache 
invalidates on new candle close. Stale cache never returned after TTL. DetectDataGaps Inputs: candles array, 
expected_interval. Checks consecutive timestamps differ by interval. Returns [{gap_start, gap_end, duration}]. Empty 
array if no gaps. Injected missing candle flagged. Two consecutive missing candles both flagged. No gaps produces empty 
array. 2.3 Tick processing Function Rule / Logic Test Criteria AggregateTicksByPrice Inputs: ticks array, bucket_size 
(0.1% of price). Groups ticks into buckets. Returns {bucket_centre: {count, volume, buy_volume, sell_volume}}. Sum of 
all tick volumes in bucket equals raw tick volume at that price. Total volume across buckets equals total input volume. 
CalculateTickDensity Inputs: aggregated_ticks, window_seconds. Returns {price_level: density_score}. Density = 
count/window_seconds. Normalised 0-1 across all levels. Level with 100 ticks scores higher than level with 10 ticks. 
Density decays to zero outside window. Max score = 1. DetectTickCluster Inputs: tick_density, threshold (0.7). Returns 
[{price_centre, density_score, width}] for all levels above threshold. Synthetic cluster at known price detected. 
Random ticks spread across prices at same volume = no cluster. Threshold boundary tested. CalculateVolumeDelta Inputs: 
ticks array, price_bucket. Returns {delta: buy-sell, total_volume, delta_ratio}. Positive = more aggressive buying. 
Manual buy minus sell sum matches output. Positive delta in known uptrend. Negative in known downtrend. 
DetectAbsorption Inputs: volume_delta, price_movement_pct. Returns {absorbed: bool, side, strength}. Absorbed when 
delta &gt; threshold AND price_movement &lt; 0.1%. Large volume + minimal price movement = absorbed. Large volume + 
significant price movement = not absorbed. MeasureBidAskSpread Inputs: order_book. Returns {spread_absolute, 
spread_pct, mid_price}. Monitors 60-second rolling window for tightening signal. Spread matches manual top-of-book 
calculation. Tightening signal fires when spread narrows &gt;20% over 60 seconds. CalculateCumulativeVolumeDelta 
Inputs: volume_delta stream, window. Returns running sum. Resets on session open. Divergence flag fires when CVD makes 
new high/low but price does not. CVD rises in known uptrend. Divergence flag fires correctly on historical examples.

ClassifyCandle Inputs: candle {open,high,low,close}. Returns {type:ERC|NRC|DOJI}. ERC=body&gt;=75% range. 
NRC=body&lt;=50%. DOJI=body&lt;=5%. Body 80% of range = ERC. 40% = NRC. 3% = DOJI. All boundary values tested. 
MeasureWickExtreme Inputs: candles array touching a price band. Returns {wick_high:max(highs), wick_low:min(lows)}. 
Wick high matches candle high exactly. Wick low matches candle low exactly. Tested against raw OHLCV. 
MeasureBodyCluster Inputs: candles array. Returns {cluster_centre, cluster_width}. Centre = median of (open+close)/2. 
Width = 90th percentile body range. Cluster centre falls between open and close. Width covers 90% of body extremes. 
CountTouches Inputs: candles, pool_rectangle. Returns {touch_count, body_touches, wick_touches}. Touch = candle 
high&gt;=pool.low AND candle low&lt;=pool.high. Manual count matches output. Candles entirely outside zone do not 
increment. Body vs wick classified correctly. MeasureTouchQuality Inputs: candle, pool, subsequent_candles(5). Returns 
{quality_score 0-100}. Scored on rejection_speed, rejection_distance, candle_type. Strong rejection scores 80+. Weak 
touch below 30. Score monotonically related to rejection speed and distance. DetectEngulfingCandle Inputs: candle, 
pool_rectangle. Returns {engulfed:bool}. Engulfed = body crosses BOTH pool ceiling and floor. Body crosses both 
boundaries = true. Partial overlap = false. Tested on historical BTC breakout candles. DetectWickPierce Inputs: candle, 
pool_rectangle, boundary. Returns {pierced:bool, depth_pct}. Pierced = wick crosses boundary but body stays inside. 
Wick crossing boundary with body inside = true. Body crossing = false. Depth pct matches manual calculation. 
MeasureDepartureStrength Inputs: departure_candle, prior_base_candles. Returns {strength_score 0-100}. Gap-away=100. 
ERC=60. NRC=25. DOJI=5. Historical departure events classified correctly vs manual review. DetectTightening Inputs: 
recent_wicks(last 5), pool. Returns {tightening:bool, trend}. Tightening = each successive wick less deep into zone. 
Three successive tightening wicks detected. Same-depth wicks not detected. Two wicks insufficient. 2.5 Pool

function and component 4.1 Testing philosophy Three tiers: unit tests verify individual function outputs against known 
inputs. Integration tests verify functions call each other correctly. System tests verify the complete pipeline against 
real historical BTC data where correct outputs are known from manual analysis. The auto-research framework (Karpathy 
methodology) is the execution environment for all three tiers. A function that passes unit tests but fails the system 
test has a specification gap. A function that passes the system test but fails unit tests has an implementation that is 
accidentally correct and will break. The integrity score is the ultimate system test. If Zenny achieves &gt;= 80% 
integrity on a known historical day where setups are manually identified and verified, the system is working. Below 80% 
on a known-good day = a function is broken. 4.2 Unit test requirements Every function in Document 2 must have unit 
tests covering: happy path with standard inputs, boundary conditions at exact threshold values (60 not 59, 14 not 
13.9), edge cases (empty arrays, null inputs, zero values, max values), and failure cases with correct failure reasons. 
All unit tests deterministic — no randomness, no network calls, all dependencies mocked. Suite completes in &lt; 30 
seconds. 4.3 Integration test call chains FetchCandles → BuildPriceWire → SmoothWire → MeasureWireAngle → 
MapAngleToGann → ClassifyStrategy → RegimeGuard. Output: correct strategy classification for known historical 
candle sequences. FetchTicks → AggregateTicksByPrice → CalculateTickDensity → DetectTickCluster → 
DetectPoolBirth. Output: birth event at correct timestamp for known tick cluster injection. ValidateCandidatePool → 
SetPoolBoundaries → all Score* functions → AggregatePoolScore → ValidatePoolThreshold. Output: correct score and 
pass/fail for three known historical pools. DetectPoolWeakening → DetectPoolDeath → ApplyPolarityFlip → 
FilterPoolByMinimumScore. Output: correct lifecycle events for pool that weakens and dies on known historical data. 
CalculatePullScore → RankPoolsByPull → DetermineArmDominance → CheckArmExists. Output: correct dominance 
assignment for known pool pairs. MeasureApproachVelocity → ClassifyApproach → EstimateSweepDepth → 
EstimateRejectionSpeed → ClassifyTendency → EstimateGhostCandleShape → DrawUpperTendencyWire. Output: correct 
tendency wire for three known approach scenarios. 4.4 System test — known historical day Select a historical BTC day 
with at least 5 manually-identified valid setups. This is ground truth. Run full Zenny pipeline and verify: Every 
manually identified pool appears in pool list with score &gt;= 60 Every pool&apos;s wick_high and wick_low within 0.1%
